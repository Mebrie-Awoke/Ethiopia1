# Ethiopian Food

## Staple Ingredients and Flavor Profiles

Ethiopian cuisine is built around injera, a spongy flatbread made from teff flour. Teff gives injera a slightly sour taste and a soft texture. Meals are often spicy, aromatic, and richly seasoned.

## Famous Dishes

Doro wat is a hearty chicken stew cooked with berbere spice, onions, garlic, and niter kibbeh. Kitfo is spiced raw or lightly cooked minced beef served with gomen (cooked greens) and ayib (cottage cheese). Shiro is a creamy stew made from ground chickpeas or broad beans.

## Vegetarian and Fasting Cuisine

Ethiopia has a strong vegetarian tradition shaped by Orthodox fasting rules. Popular dishes include misir wat (lentil stew), atkilt wat (cabbage, carrots, and potatoes), and gomen wat (seasoned collard greens).

## Bread, Grains, and Breakfast Foods

Besides injera, Ethiopian meals may include dabo kolo (spiced toasted barley snacks), ambasha (sweet flatbread), and genfo (porridge made from barley or wheat). Breakfast often features firfir: shredded injera soaked in spicy sauce.

## Coffee and Beverages

Ethiopian coffee is world-renowned. The coffee ceremony is a communal event where freshly roasted beans are brewed and poured into small cups. Tej, a honey wine, is also part of traditional hospitality.

## Regional Variations

In the north, stews are robust and spicy. In the south, enset (false banana) is a staple crop. Coastal regions near the Red Sea offer seafood dishes with distinct flavors.

## Food Culture and Dining Rituals

Ethiopian dining emphasizes sharing from a common platter. Eating together with hands creates a sense of community. Guests are offered the first servings, and hosts take special care to ensure everyone is satisfied.
