# Ethiopian Languages

## Language Diversity and National Identity

Ethiopia is one of the most linguistically diverse countries in the world. More than 80 languages are spoken across the nation, representing several language families including Afroasiatic and Nilo-Saharan.

## Official Languages and Working Languages

Amharic has long been the federal working language, and it remains the most widely spoken language in government, business, and media. Afaan Oromo and Tigrinya are also major languages with deep cultural and literary traditions.

## Semitic Languages

Amharic, Tigrinya, and Gurage languages are part of the Semitic family. These languages use the Ge’ez script, an ancient writing system with characters that represent consonant-vowel combinations.

## Cushitic Languages

Oromo and Somali are Cushitic languages spoken by tens of millions of Ethiopians. These languages have rich oral traditions, poetry, and songs that are central to community life.

## Indigenous Language Preservation

Many smaller languages are spoken by communities in the south, west, and east. Efforts to preserve these languages include bilingual education, cultural documentation, and local media.

## Language and Cultural Expression

Language is a key element of identity in Ethiopia. Proverbs, folk stories, songs, and traditional greetings carry meaning across generations. The Ge’ez script remains a powerful symbol of the country’s written heritage.

## The Role of Language in Unity and Diversity

Ethiopia balances national unity with regional diversity. Languages are celebrated as expressions of history and culture while helping preserve local knowledge and traditions.
